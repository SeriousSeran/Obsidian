# Community Acquired Pneumonia

> **Domain:** Medicine | **Status:** Active | **Review:** Pending
> Not final clinical advice. Learning material only.

---

## Definition

> What is this condition/concept in one clear sentence?

**Definition ::** An acute infection of the lung parenchyma acquired outside of a hospital setting, typically presenting with fever, cough, and radiological evidence of consolidation.

---

## Pathophysiology 🔬

> Mechanism — what goes wrong at the cellular or physiological level?

**Pathophysiology ::** Microorganisms (usually bacteria or viruses) bypass upper airway defenses or are microaspirated, reaching the alveoli where they multiply. This triggers an inflammatory cascade, leading to alveolar exudate, consolidation, and impaired gas exchange.

---

## Clinical Features 🩺

### Symptoms
- Cough (often productive of purulent or rust-colored sputum)
- Fever, chills, or rigors
- Pleuritic chest pain
- Dyspnea
- Fatigue, myalgia, confusion (especially in the elderly)

### Signs
- Tachypnea, tachycardia, hypoxia
- Dullness to percussion
- Bronchial breathing
- Coarse crackles
- Increased vocal resonance (e.g., egophony, tactile fremitus)

### Investigations
| Test | Expected Finding | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Chest X-ray (CXR) | Lobar or patchy consolidation, pleural effusion | Confirms diagnosis and extent |
| Blood tests (FBC, CRP, U&E) | Leukocytosis (neutrophilia), elevated CRP, AKI | Assess severity, guide CURB-65 |
| Sputum culture | Causative organism (e.g., S. pneumoniae) | Guides targeted antibiotic therapy |
| Blood cultures | Bacteremia | Useful in severe cases or non-responders |

**Key investigation ::** Chest X-ray (CXR)

---

## Diagnosis

**Gold standard:**
> Clinical presentation consistent with lower respiratory tract infection PLUS new focal signs on chest examination and new consolidation on chest radiography.

**Gold standard investigation ::** Chest X-ray (CXR)

**Clinical criteria / scoring:**
- **CURB-65 Score** (predicts 30-day mortality):
  - **C**onfusion (AMTS <= 8 or new disorientation)
  - **U**rea > 7 mmol/L
  - **R**espiratory rate >= 30/min
  - **B**lood pressure (systolic < 90 mmHg or diastolic <= 60 mmHg)
  - Age >= **65** years

---

## Management 💊

### Acute
1. **Oxygen Therapy:** Titrate to target SpO2 (e.g., 94-98% for most, 88-92% if at risk of type 2 respiratory failure).
2. **Intravenous Fluids:** Resuscitation or maintenance if hypotensive or unable to tolerate oral intake.
3. **Empiric Antibiotics:** Based on local guidelines and severity (CURB-65 score).

### Long-term
- Smoking cessation advice
- Pneumococcal and influenza vaccination (especially in vulnerable groups)
- Follow-up CXR at 6 weeks to ensure resolution (exclude underlying malignancy, particularly in older patients or smokers)

### Medications
| Drug | Dose | Indication | Key Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amoxicillin | e.g., 500mg TDS PO | Mild CAP (typical) | GI upset, rash, anaphylaxis |
| Doxycycline / Macrolide | Variable | Penicillin allergy or atypical suspicion | Photosensitivity, QT prolongation (macrolides) |
| Co-amoxiclav + Clarithromycin | Variable | Severe CAP (CURB-65 3-5) | Hepatotoxicity, severe GI upset |

**First-line treatment ::** Empiric antibiotics depending on severity (e.g., Amoxicillin for mild CAP).

---

## Complications ⚠️

- Parapneumonic effusion and empyema
- Lung abscess
- Sepsis / Septic shock
- Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

**Most serious complication ::** Sepsis / Septic shock leading to multi-organ failure.

---

## Viva Points 🎯

> What examiners love to ask about this topic.

**Viva Q — Classic presentation ::** Typical lobar pneumonia with acute onset fever, productive cough, pleuritic chest pain, and signs of consolidation.

**Viva Q — Why this investigation? ::** Sputum culture allows identification of the causative organism and guides targeted antibiotic therapy, especially if initial empiric treatment fails.

**Viva Q — Management priority ::** Early administration of appropriate empiric antibiotics and oxygenation.

**What is Community Acquired Pneumonia? ::** An acute infection of the lung parenchyma acquired outside of a hospital setting, typically presenting with fever, cough, and radiological evidence of consolidation.

## OSCE Tips

- In a respiratory exam, explicitly mention that you are listening for bronchial breathing and vocal resonance over areas of suspected consolidation.
- Always offer to check O2 saturations, respiratory rate, and temperature.
