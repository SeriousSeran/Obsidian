# Deidentified Clinical Case: Acute Asthma Exacerbation

## Presentation
A 22-year-old female presents to the Emergency Department with a 2-day history of progressively worsening shortness of breath and wheezing. She reports a dry cough, mostly at night, and states that her albuterol inhaler has not provided relief. She has a known history of asthma since childhood.

## Findings
- **Vitals:** RR 28, HR 115, SpO2 93% on room air, Temp 37.0°C.
- **Chest Exam:** Bilateral widespread expiratory wheeze. Accessory muscle use visible. Able to speak in partial sentences.
- **Other:** No peripheral edema, no clubbing.

## Assessment
Acute severe asthma exacerbation. The presence of tachycardia, tachypnea, and inability to complete full sentences points to a severe exacerbation rather than moderate.

## Learning Points
- Always assess severity based on standardized criteria (e.g., speech, HR, RR, PEF if available).
- Immediate administration of high-flow oxygen, back-to-back nebulized bronchodilators, and systemic corticosteroids is the cornerstone of initial management.
- Reassessment is crucial to determine if escalation of care (e.g., IV magnesium sulfate or intubation) is necessary.

## OSCE/Viva Connection
- **OSCE:** Respiratory examination station, managing an acutely breathless patient.
- **Viva:** Discuss the criteria for life-threatening vs. severe asthma.

## Follow Up
Patient responded well to initial nebulizers and IV hydrocortisone. Symptoms improved, and she was transitioned to oral prednisolone and discharged the following day with an updated asthma action plan and a scheduled follow-up with her primary care physician.
