# Asthma Exacerbation

## Presentation
A young adult presented to the emergency department with a 2-day history of progressively worsening shortness of breath, wheeze, and a non-productive cough. They had a known history of asthma but had run out of their preventer inhaler 2 weeks ago. Reported feeling tight in the chest and unable to complete sentences without pausing for breath.

## Findings
On examination, the patient was tachycardic (HR 115) and tachypnoeic (RR 26). Oxygen saturations were 93% on room air. Inspection revealed use of accessory muscles. Auscultation of the chest revealed widespread bilateral expiratory wheeze. PEFR was recorded at 40% of their predicted best.

## Assessment
Acute severe asthma exacerbation, likely triggered by non-adherence to preventer medication or a mild upper respiratory tract viral infection.

## Learning Points
- Initial management priority is ABCDE, specifically high-flow oxygen, nebulized salbutamol, and systemic corticosteroids (e.g., oral prednisolone or IV hydrocortisone).
- Close monitoring of respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, heart rate, and PEFR is critical.
- A normal or rising PaCO2 in an acute asthma attack is a dangerous sign indicating exhaustion and potential need for escalation to intensive care.

## Follow Up
Patient responded well to initial therapy in the ED. Discharged 48 hours later after stabilization, with a short course of oral corticosteroids, optimization of inhaler technique, and a scheduled follow-up with their general practitioner.
