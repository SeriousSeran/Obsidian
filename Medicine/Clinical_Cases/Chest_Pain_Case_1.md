# Deidentified Clinical Case: Acute Coronary Syndrome

## Presentation
- 65-year-old male presenting to the emergency department with central, crushing chest pain.
- Pain started 2 hours ago while resting, radiating to the left arm and jaw.
- Accompanied by diaphoresis and nausea.
- Past Medical History: Hypertension, Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, 30-pack-year smoking history.

## Findings
- **Observations:** HR 105, BP 155/90, RR 22, SpO2 96% on room air.
- **Examination:** Patient appears pale, sweaty, and distressed. Heart sounds dual, no murmurs. Chest clear to auscultation.
- **ECG:** ST elevation in leads II, III, and aVF (Inferior STEMI). Reciprocal changes in I and aVL.

## Assessment
- Acute Inferior ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI).
- High priority for immediate reperfusion therapy.

## Learning Points
- Immediate management includes aspirin 300mg, clopidogrel 600mg (or alternative P2Y12 inhibitor per local guidelines), GTN, IV morphine + metoclopramide if in severe pain, and emergency cardiology referral for Primary PCI.
- Recognize reciprocal ST depression as a confirmatory sign of STEMI in the presence of ST elevation.
- Right ventricular involvement is common in inferior STEMIs; avoid nitrates if patient becomes hypotensive, and consider right-sided ECG leads (V4R).

## Follow Up
- Requires secondary prevention: Aspirin, second antiplatelet (e.g. Ticagrelor), Beta-blocker, ACE inhibitor, and high-dose statin.
- Cardiac rehabilitation referral.
- Lifestyle modification advice (smoking cessation, diet, exercise).
